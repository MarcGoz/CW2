Protect the Environment, Protect Wildlife

The illicit trade in wildlife is a formidable global issue that poses an existential threat to countless species and undermining conservation efforts. This nefarious commerce encompasses the unlawful pursuit, smuggling, and vending of animals - including those on the brink of extinction - with estimated annual earnings between $7-23 billion dollars. According to the Global Environment Facility (2019), it's one amongst many transnational operations classified under organized crime.

Approximately 350 million living organisms are purchased or sold illegally annually as per the

Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora (2022).

This illicit exchange has led to the decrease in numerous species, which was evident when the population of African elephants quickly dropped by around 111,000 within just ten years, due to poaching (IUCN, 2019). Additionally, it has been seen that illegal pet trade has moved to online platforms like Facebook and Instagram, with 8,508 ivory items posted for sale on 1,559 accounts in Indonesia, Thailand, and Vietnam in 2016 (TRAFFIC, n.d.).

Dealing with this unlawful trade is obligatory not only to save our natural surroundings but also as a responsibility of the government. We need more than just laws against illegal activities; it necessitates strong political determination and cooperation. We need programs that tackle education and awareness geared toward modifying consumer behaviors that could greatly decrease the demand for illicit animal products.

Illegal wild trafficking is a complex subject that involves economic systems, social norms, culture, and even politics. To combat said issue, we must:

  • Strengthen the law by punishing people for poaching, trafficking, and trading illegal wildlife and preventing incentives for reporting wildlife-related crimes.
  • Make illegal wildlife trafficking a national issue, which may lead to increased funding for wildlife conservation and collaborating with other countries on wildlife protection initiatives.
  • Apprehend politicians and other key players involved in the trade by increasing transparency, accountability, and anti-corruption movements.
  • Raise awareness and provide alternative livelihoods to those involved in the trade to reduce the number of illegal traders.

To conclude, ending illegal wildlife trafficking requires a holistic approach that includes politicians, the law, and the community to eradicate the root causes of the issue. By working together, we can protect the beauty of wildlife for us and future generations.
